1) Explain why 0.999... equals 1.
Answer:
The repeating decimal 0.999... is exactly equal to 1. This might seem counterintuitive at first, but it's a mathematically proven fact, not just an approximation.

Here are a couple of common ways to understand why:

1.  **Algebraic Proof:**
    *   Let `S = 0.999...`
    *   Multiply both sides by 10: `10S = 9.999...`
    *   Subtract the first equation from the second: `10S - S = 9.999... - 0.999...`
    *   This simplifies to `9S = 9`
    *   Divide by 9: `S = 1`

2.  **Fraction Conversion:**
    *   You know that `1/9 = 0.111...`
    *   If you multiply both sides of this equation by 9, you get `9 * (1/9) = 9 * (0.111...)`
    *   This results in `1 = 0.999...`

Essentially, 0.999... and 1 represent the exact same number. It's not "almost 1" or "very, very nearly 1"; it *is* 1.

3) What is the difference between correlation and causation?
Answer:
Correlation and causation are two distinct concepts used to describe relationships between variables. 

**Correlation** indicates that a relationship exists between two events or variables, meaning they tend to change together. For example, if variable X increases, variable Y also tends to increase, or vice versa. However, correlation does not imply that one causes the other.

**Causation**, on the other hand, means that one event directly causes another. It indicates a cause-and-effect relationship where a change in one variable directly leads to a change in another.

It's a common misconception that correlation equals causation. While two variables might be correlated, it doesn't necessarily mean one caused the other; there could be other factors at play or it could be a coincidence.

4) Why does dividing by zero break mathematics?
Answer:
Dividing by zero breaks mathematics because it leads to contradictions and undefined results, which are incompatible with the logical and consistent framework that mathematics relies upon. If we were to allow division by zero, we could prove false statements to be true, undermining the entire system of mathematical reasoning.
